SPACE DWELLERS

PROBLEM

Dolls are cherished by people of various ages, but there exists many stereotypes around dolls. Some see them as merely comfort objects, dress up mannequins, or props for dreaming fantasy. Children might love the imaginative freedom, and need the social skills and empathy that doll play helps to build; but they could also feel pressured by peers and adults to play other toys that are designed for cognitive skill development.

How can doll play be enhanced to counter the stereotypes, while maintaining the imaginative and comforting aspect of them?

RESEARCH & IDEATION

As a starting point, I knew I wanted to design characters and a complete environment; thus, I did some market research on doll house and similar toys on the market, and try to find the vacuum in the current market.

The research let me know that "a doll house like toy" can go into many trajectories, and I wasn't sure where I wanted to go. Therefore, I looked at various relevant toys on the market, and tried to rate each kind out of 5 based on the criteria below.

This process helped me understand while a lot of toys score high on cognitive and fine motor development, their non-dictated play score would be lower due to their complexity. My opportunity would be to design a toy that allows children to make something intricate, but also gives them creative freedom and an end result to actually play with. I sketched some rough ideas.

DESIGNING THE DOLL

I wanted to design the characters before designing where they live. I began prototyping humanoid formed aliens; being similar to humans would help children relate to them, while their ambiguous alien form allows children to imagine the gender, characteristic, and personality of their dolls.

Due to the intricate construction, the doll would be manufactured. Yet, children could pick and even make their own wigs and clothing for their dolls. Since they are aliens, they could have non-natural skin colors.

DESIGNING THE ENVIRONMENT

The environment is the construction part of this toy. The idea I eventually landed on is to have "paper folding but in fabrics." The soft and layered folding pieces would allow the final folded products to look like real furniture. During the folding process, children get to explore how to work with soft materials, and also strengthen their 3D visualization and crafting skills.

The playing method is intuitive: fold it, roll it, flip it, tie it (string, elastic, ribbon ...), and clip it (paper clips, binder clips, hair clips ...)!

To test playability, I explored folding some "furniture" with this mechanism, and see if it's expansive.

Because the material is soft and one continuous surface, the furniture display a fluid and futuristic aesthetic that aligns with the alien theme. Children can interpret the function of what they folded, which encourages the mentality that imagination goes beyond preconception.
